∙∙1930s∙∙

The term “gold,” oftentimes referring to stories about moving off the gold standard, spiked in usage during the 1930s, one of the key national political debates in the early years of The Great Depression.

In local coverage, Fritz Crisler signed on as Head Football Coach in 1932 — the first football coach in sixty-three years to not be a Princeton alumni. He would go on to coach successfully for six years, including two undefeated seasons, until he moved to the University of Michigan, a decision the Prince’s regular sports analysis column, Time Out, called “regrettable” but understandable given that Crisler had “no really intimate contact with Princeton.”

Although Princeton had been playing intramural sports for decades, coverage of intramural sports rose sharply in the 1930s, with game schedules and outcomes printed regularly. The program was so popular that James Tyson, then scribe at The Harvard Crimson, wrote in to praise it compared to Harvard’s much smaller intramural league.

The 1930s also saw the opening of McCarter Theatre, designed and financed by the Triangle Club and made possible by a $250,000 gift from alumni Thomas N. McCarter '88

Usage of the word “geology” also peaked, used oftentimes in reference to “an unprecedented array” of field work trips to locations including The West Indies, Yellowstone, and Canada.
